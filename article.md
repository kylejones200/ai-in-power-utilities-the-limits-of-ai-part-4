### AI in Power & Utilities: The Limits of AI (part 4) The pace of progress in artificial intelligence has created a sense

that the technology can do anything. In reality, it cannot. Models are getting better at solving well-defined problems, answering factual questions, and generating fluent text. They are improving in image recognition, audio transcription, and sequence modeling. These are genuine achievements. At the same time, the systems remain limited. They struggle with planning, abstraction, and reasoning across steps. They cannot explain their logic. They cannot verify their own answers. They often respond with confidence, even when they are wrong. These limitations affect performance in ways that matter. A model that misinterprets a sequence of events may recommend the wrong maintenance schedule. A model that cannot distinguish between similar asset types may assign the wrong risk level. A model that fills in missing data with plausible guesses may create the appearance of accuracy while masking uncertainty. These behaviors make AI systems brittle. One area where models struggle most is planning. Not high-level forecasting or resource allocation, but basic planning. Ask a language model to generate a sequence of actions --- how to assemble parts, how to shut down a system, how to prioritize repairs --- and it will often fail. It will drop steps. It will misorder dependencies. It will repeat actions that should only happen once. These mistakes reveal the gap between language fluency and operational logic. In testing environments, models routinely underperform on planning benchmarks. They score well on knowledge recall. They score lower on tasks that require working memory, conditional logic, or causal inference. This is because the underlying architectures are not designed for those functions. They predict the next token based on statistical likelihood. They do not simulate a world. They do not track intent. They do not build mental models. These gaps do not disappear with more data. They require different approaches. Another limitation is abstraction. When presented with tasks that require pattern recognition across examples, models can perform well. When asked to generalize across domains or analogize from limited input, they often fail. They do not reason through metaphor. They do not reframe problems. They do not synthesize across systems. These skills remain uniquely human. Visual reasoning is another weak point. While models can describe images or detect patterns, they struggle with context. Ask a model to explain why a pole is leaning, and it may describe the image without understanding the cause. Ask it to evaluate a heat map and it may describe the colors without linking them to asset health. Interpretation remains shallow. The model sees pixels, not problems. Perhaps the most persistent limitation is reliability. AI systems do not know when they are wrong. They do not signal uncertainty. They do not reject invalid tasks. They often prefer to produce something rather than nothing. This creates risk. In safety-critical settings, a confident wrong answer is more dangerous than no answer at all. This behavior is built into the training process. Models are rewarded for being helpful and complete. They are penalized for refusing to answer. They are optimized to sound fluent. These incentives encourage overreach. They do not align with the expectations of regulated environments. When applied to power systems, this creates a mismatch. Operators need systems that can explain themselves. They need systems that reflect physical constraints. They need systems that defer when they lack information. These traits do not yet describe most AI tools. Another failure point is traceability. Most language models cannot explain how they reached a conclusion. They cannot point to which data informed a decision. They cannot reconstruct their reasoning. This makes it difficult to audit outputs. It makes it difficult to correct errors. It limits the ability to improve performance over time. Models are also vulnerable to prompt injection, adversarial examples, and data poisoning. These attacks do not require deep access. They exploit the openness of the model. They exploit its willingness to complete tasks without verifying source or intent. These weaknesses become critical when AI is used to generate code, configure systems, or recommend actions. In short, the model may sound like an expert. It is not one. It has no memory of past tasks unless that memory is engineered. It has no goals. It has no concept of risk. It has no awareness of what it does not know. This is why guardrails matter. The most important protection is not a feature. It is context. When systems are built with structured data, clear definitions, version control, and access management, the model has boundaries. It cannot invent values. It cannot fabricate context. It must work with what it is given. This is also why human oversight is essential. AI can generate first drafts. It can summarize trends. It can extract meaning from logs. It cannot replace judgment. It cannot replace the understanding of a field technician or the discretion of a dispatcher. It can augment the work. It cannot own the outcome. These limits are not flaws to be fixed tomorrow. They are structural. They will take years of research to overcome. They may never be fully resolved. The work now is to understand them and design around them. If the model cannot plan, then pair it with a planning system. If the model cannot reason, then limit it to interpretation. If the model cannot self-check, then route its outputs through validation layers. This is not a weakness. This is engineering. No one would ask an intern to operate a substation alone. No one would expect a junior analyst to write policy without review. The same standard should apply to AI. Treat it as a contributor. Give it tasks that fit. Keep it accountable. AI will improve. That is certain. It will get faster. It will access more modalities. It will integrate into more tools. Even so, its limits will remain a critical part of the design space. Knowing what AI cannot do is as important as knowing what it can.
### **Internal Pressure, External Demand** Artificial intelligence does not arrive quietly. It brings with it new questions about risk, ownership, and control.

These questions rarely appear on a roadmap. They appear in meetings, procurement requests, data access forms, compliance checklists, and budget reviews. They come from within the organization and from outside it. They multiply as adoption spreads. Internally, the pressure begins with interest. Teams want to experiment. They see what other companies are doing. They want to keep pace. They start small. A chatbot here. A report summarizer there. Then they ask for access to more data. They want longer time windows, broader tables, richer text. They ask for permissions that did not exist before. They want logs, documents, images, and code. They want to move quickly. At first, these requests sound reasonable. After all, the tools are available. The interfaces are easy. A few lines of code can connect a model to a data warehouse. A few clicks can enable an API. The technology makes it possible to deploy intelligence across departments. Marketing wants summarization. Legal wants document review. Customer support wants faster response. Operations wants predictive alerts. Each case seems justified. Over time, the organization begins to lose track. Who is using which model? Where is the data coming from? What prompts are being issued? What outputs are being stored? What decisions are being made downstream? These questions surface slowly. They arrive after something breaks, or after someone outside the organization asks for an explanation. The answers are often unclear. This is the moment when internal pressure shifts into something more serious. Executives realize that access to models also means exposure. Data that was previously locked in applications is now being queried by systems that do not enforce boundaries. Models that produce summaries can also produce inferences. They can reveal more than intended. They can synthesize details into insights that cross privacy thresholds or compliance lines. Once a model touches sensitive data, governance must catch up. That means understanding where the data flows. It means tracking who issued the query and what output was generated. It means documenting how the model was trained, how it was fine-tuned, and what instructions were given. These layers of traceability are not optional. They are the foundation of defensible operations. The pressure does not stay inside. It expands outward. External actors begin to take notice. Vendors ask for access to operational records. They want to improve their models using real-world data. They offer incentives. They promise insights in return. Some requests are valuable. Some are extractive. The distinction is not always obvious. Regulators follow. They want to understand how AI is used. They ask how decisions are made. They ask whether models are tested, validated, and monitored. They want proof that automation does not create bias, error, or harm. They want audit logs. They want policies. They want to know who is responsible. Partners and suppliers raise their own questions. They ask about integration. They ask about compatibility. They want to know whether your AI will work with theirs. They want to exchange data. They want shared access to systems. They want alignment, even if your timelines or standards are different. Customers raise expectations too. They assume that if AI is being used, service will improve. They want faster response. They want personalization. They want explanation. If something goes wrong, they want to know whether the mistake came from a person or a machine. They want transparency without technical complexity. They want confidence that the system acts in their interest. This convergence creates a new layer of responsibility. Utilities must now manage AI as part of their infrastructure. Not as a pilot. Not as a tool for a single team. As a system that intersects with every function, every policy, and every relationship. To succeed, this system must rest on clear principles. Data access must be intentional. Models must be scoped. Outputs must be evaluated. When AI is applied to sensitive or safety-critical tasks, oversight must be structured and recorded. Informal experiments must become formal processes. This does not mean every model needs a review board. It means that models should not appear in production without visibility. It means that when a system starts making decisions, there should be a person who understands how it works. There should be a record of when it was last evaluated. There should be a plan for what happens if it fails. The same logic applies to external demand. When a partner asks for data, the organization must ask what will be built with it. When a vendor offers a model, the organization must ask how it was trained. When a regulator asks for documentation, the organization must be ready to provide it. These requirements are not constraints. They are signs of maturity. They reflect the fact that AI is no longer speculative. It is operational. It shapes outcomes. It creates exposure. It requires governance. In this context, data becomes more than an asset. It becomes a surface of interaction. Each record can be queried, summarized, or inferred. Each dataset can become part of a training run. Each system can become a source of prompts. That is why control matters. That is why definitions, labels, and permissions must be maintained. The internal pressure to innovate will not go away. The external demand for access will increase. Utilities must stand at the center with clarity. They must know what data they have, what models they use, what outputs they trust, and what risks they are prepared to accept.
